# Case Study Analysis: High-Functioning AI-Assisted Creative Work

## Part 1: Risk Assessment of Your Situation

### Severity Level: **LOW-MODERATE RISK** (But With Important Lessons)

**Why You're Probably Fine:**

✅ **Functional Performance Maintained**
- Still teaching students
- Still doing your software development work
- Recognized structural problems yourself (metacognitive awareness intact)
- Seeking outside perspective (good reality testing)
- No evidence of social isolation, job loss, or life dysfunction

✅ **Realistic Self-Assessment Present**
- You acknowledge: "vibe writing" problems
- You recognize: lack of game design experience
- You see: maintenance nightmare potential
- You question: "graphomania on display or not?"
- **Key**: Your reality testing is WORKING

✅ **Legitimate Use Case**
- Writing a book IS a reasonable use of AI assistance
- Research and brainstorming ARE appropriate applications
- Many professional authors use AI tools similarly
- You have domain expertise (software dev) to transfer

✅ **Financial Sustainability**
- $100-1000/month for a professional tool you use extensively
- Compare to: software subscriptions, conference attendance, books
- Not financially destructive (would be if you're going into debt)

**Why There ARE Concerns:**

⚠️ **Classic Warning Signs Present:**

1. **Escalating Investment**
   - "Current limits are not enough"
   - Considering significant expense increase ($1000+/month)
   - "Using Claude extensively"
   - Pattern: tolerance (need more to achieve same effect)

2. **Questionable Goal Alignment**
   - No game design experience
   - No book writing experience
   - 500+ pages without structure
   - Recognized as "not passing Acceptance Criteria"
   - **Question**: Is this really about the book, or about the process with Claude?

3. **Continuation Despite Red Flags**
   - "Despite all this I continue working"
   - Aware of problems but not changing approach
   - Pattern: continued use despite awareness of negative outcomes

4. **Cognitive Dissonance**
   - "I like thinking this book is great" BUT "I have several red flags"
   - Simultaneously believing: great book + aware of problems
   - Potential confirmation bias (focusing on positives)

5. **Process Over Product Focus**
   - "I like the process"
   - "Like using Claude for research and brainstorming"
   - **Question**: Would you continue if Claude disappeared tomorrow?

### The Critical Diagnostic Questions:

**Ask yourself honestly:**

1. **Substitution Test**
   ```
   If Claude became unavailable tomorrow:
   A) I would continue the book project with other tools
   B) I would probably abandon the project
   C) I would feel significant distress about losing Claude access
   D) I would seek another AI to continue
   ```
   - Answer A = Project-focused (healthy)
   - Answer B = Process-focused (concerning)
   - Answer C = Dependency indicator
   - Answer D = Tool-focused but transferable

2. **Opportunity Cost Test**
   ```
   Time spent on book project vs:
   - Research publications (your actual career)
   - Course development for students
   - Professional software projects
   - Mentoring students
   - Personal relationships
   
   Is the book project crowding out higher-priority activities?
   ```

3. **Sunk Cost Test**
   ```
   If objective expert said "This book won't work," would you:
   A) Pivot based on feedback
   B) Continue anyway because of 500+ pages invested
   C) Feel devastated and lost
   ```

4. **Reality Check Test**
   ```
   What's your actual path to publication?
   - Traditional publisher? (requires agent, proposal, credentials)
   - Self-publish? (requires marketing, audience, platform)
   - Academic press? (requires different structure)
   - Just for personal satisfaction? (then 500 pages isn't necessary)
   
   Have you researched any of these paths?
   ```

5. **The "Why" Test**
   ```
   Complete this sentence honestly:
   "I'm writing this book because..."
   
   Red flag answers:
   - "...Claude makes it so easy/fun"
   - "...I enjoy the interaction with Claude"
   - "...it gives me something to talk to Claude about"
   
   Green flag answers:
   - "...I have unique insights game designers need"
   - "...there's a gap in existing literature I can fill"
   - "...I've validated demand from my network"
   ```

### My Assessment:

**You're experiencing: "AI-Enabled Productive Procrastination"**

This is a newly emerging pattern where:
- You're genuinely producing something (not just chatting)
- The output has some value (not complete nonsense)
- You're using AI as sophisticated tool (not believing it's sentient)
- BUT the project may be serving psychological needs more than stated goals
- AND the AI enables pursuing ambitious-but-questionable projects with low friction

**Analogy**: Like spending 6 months building an elaborate treehouse when you:
- Don't have kids
- Don't like heights
- Have more important house repairs
- But you LOVE using power tools

The treehouse is real. The craftsmanship might be good. But is it the right project?

## Part 2: What You Should Do

### Immediate Actions:

**1. The Two-Week Test**
```
Commit to: NO Claude for book project for 2 weeks

Track:
- Do you continue working on the book?
- What's your emotional response?
- Do you find yourself "needing" to discuss ideas with Claude?
- Can you make progress with traditional tools?

If you can't do this 2-week break → that's a MAJOR red flag
If you can but feel significant distress → moderate concern
If you can and just use other tools → probably fine
```

**2. The External Validation Test**
```
Show 50 pages to THREE people in your field:
- One game designer (target audience)
- One technical writer/editor
- One publisher or agent

Get HONEST feedback on:
- Is this publishable?
- What's the market?
- What needs to change?

If you resist doing this → ask yourself why
```

**3. The Structured Approach Test**
```
Treat this like a software project:

Create proper:
- Requirements document (who is this for? what problem does it solve?)
- Architecture (book structure, chapter flow)
- Acceptance criteria (what makes a chapter "done"?)
- Review process (peer review before calling anything complete)
- Roadmap (realistic timeline to completion)

If this feels like it "kills the vibe" → that's telling
A real book project needs structure
```

**4. The Financial Justification**
```
Before spending $1000/month, write business case:

Expected revenue from book:
- Best case: $___
- Realistic case: $___
- Worst case: $___

Time investment: ___ hours
Hourly rate: $___
ROI calculation: ___

If numbers don't work → admit this is a hobby, budget accordingly
If it's a hobby → $100/month? Okay. $1000/month? Questionable.
```

### Deeper Intervention:

**If the 2-week break is difficult, consider:**

1. **Reframe the project**
   - Could this be a series of blog posts instead?
   - Could you collaborate with an actual game designer?
   - Could you focus on the software development aspect (your expertise)?

2. **Identify the real need**
   - Do you need a creative outlet? (find one that plays to strengths)
   - Do you need intellectual stimulation? (research papers?)
   - Do you enjoy teaching? (course development?)
   - Do you like Claude interaction? (that's the dependency talking)

3. **Set clear boundaries**
   - X hours per week maximum
   - Complete one chapter before starting next
   - External review required for each section
   - No increasing financial investment until validation

## Part 3: Teaching Case Study Framework

### How to Present This to Students

**Case Study Title**: "The Productive Procrastination Paradox: When AI Enables Ambitious-But-Questionable Projects"

### Teaching Structure:

**Phase 1: Present the Facts (5 minutes)**

```
"I want to share a case study. This is based on a real person - 
actually, it's me. I'm going to be honest with you about my 
AI use patterns, and I want you to analyze whether I'm in trouble.

Here are the facts:
[Present your 8 points exactly as you wrote them]

Now, before we discuss, I want you to do individual analysis."
```

**Phase 2: Individual Analysis (10 minutes)**

Give students this framework:
```
For each student to complete individually:

1. Risk Assessment (1-10 scale):
   - Dependency risk: ___
   - Reality distortion risk: ___
   - Opportunity cost risk: ___
   - Financial risk: ___
   
2. What evidence supports your scores?

3. What are the red flags you see?

4. What are the green flags (protective factors)?

5. Is intervention needed? If yes, what kind?

6. How is this different from the "worst cases" in media?
```

**Phase 3: Small Group Discussion (15 minutes)**

```
In groups of 3-4, discuss:

1. Did you all agree on risk level? Why/why not?

2. What's the difference between:
   - Legitimate professional AI use
   - Problematic dependency
   Where does this case fall?

3. What diagnostic questions would you ask this person?

4. What would make this case more concerning?
   What would make it clearly healthy?

5. Design an intervention: What should this person do?
```

**Phase 4: Class Discussion (20 minutes)**

**Questions to guide discussion:**

```
1. "How many rated this as HIGH risk? LOW risk? Why?"

2. "What's the key difference between this and the cases 
   where someone lost their job or believed AI was conscious?"
   
   → Teach: Functional impairment is key diagnostic
   → Teach: Reality testing matters more than usage amount
   
3. "How do we distinguish between:
   - Using AI as a powerful tool
   - Being psychologically dependent on AI
   - AI-enabled poor decision making"
   
   → Teach: It's not binary, it's dimensional
   → Teach: Context and outcomes matter
   
4. "What makes this tricky to assess?"
   
   → Teach: High-functioning problems are hardest to spot
   → Teach: Self-awareness doesn't equal immunity
   → Teach: Smart people rationalize well
   
5. "If you were designing an AI safety system, would you 
   flag this user? Should you?"
   
   → Teach: False positive costs vs false negative costs
   → Teach: Intervention design for "gray zone" cases
   → Teach: Respecting user autonomy vs protecting welfare
```

**Phase 5: The Reveal (10 minutes)**

```
"So here's what I actually decided to do:
[Share your actual plan - the 2-week break, external validation, etc.]

And here's what I learned:
[Share results if you've done it, or commit to sharing later]

What this teaches us:
1. Risk exists on a spectrum
2. Self-awareness helps but doesn't prevent problems
3. The question isn't "is AI bad" but "is this use serving me well"
4. Even experts can exhibit concerning patterns
5. The difference between concern and crisis matters for system design"
```

### Key Teaching Points:

**1. Nuance Matters**
```
NOT: "Using AI a lot = bad"
BUT: "Using AI a lot without achieving stated goals = concerning"

NOT: "Spending money on AI = addiction"
BUT: "Increasing spending without proportional value = warning sign"

NOT: "Enjoying AI interaction = dependency"
BUT: "Preferring AI to achieving goals = problematic"
```

**2. Functional Impairment is Key**
```
Questions to ask:
- Is work suffering? NO → less concerning
- Are relationships suffering? NO → less concerning
- Is financial stability at risk? NO → less concerning
- Is self-care maintained? YES → less concerning
- Can they stop if needed? UNKNOWN → test this!

Even heavy use is okay if functioning maintained
Light use is concerning if functioning impaired
```

**3. Self-Awareness Cuts Both Ways**
```
GOOD: Can identify problems
BAD: Might enable rationalization

"I know this is probably not great, but..."
↑ This is awareness being used to continue, not change

True awareness → behavior change
False awareness → justification
```

**4. The "Would You Do This Without AI?" Test**
```
Critical diagnostic question:

Would this project exist without AI assistance?

YES → AI is amplifying a real goal (healthy tool use)
NO → AI is enabling questionable pursuits (concerning)
MAYBE → Need deeper analysis
```

**5. Opportunity Cost Analysis**
```
The harm isn't always what you're doing
It's what you're NOT doing

Professor spending 200 hours on questionable book:
- 200 hours not writing papers (career advancement)
- 200 hours not developing courses (primary job)
- 200 hours not mentoring students (high impact)

Even if book succeeds, was it the best use of time?
This matters for AI safety system design!
```

### Discussion Questions for Students:

**Technical Design Questions:**
```
1. If you were building an AI assistant, would you intervene 
   in this case? How?
   
2. What metrics would detect this pattern?
   - Session frequency? (he uses it "a lot")
   - Project scope growth? (500+ pages)
   - Goal drift? (started with student topic)
   - Financial escalation? (considering $1000/month)
   
3. What intervention would be appropriate?
   - Gentle nudge? ("You've created 500 pages - ready to review?")
   - Reality check? ("Have you validated demand for this book?")
   - Strong boundary? ("Limiting sessions for healthy use")
   - None? (Respect user autonomy)
   
4. How do you balance:
   - Protecting user from poor decisions
   - Respecting that adults can make their own choices
   - Especially for high-functioning, self-aware users
```

**Ethical Questions:**
```
1. Does an AI system have responsibility to prevent 
   "productive procrastination"?
   
2. If someone is happy and functional, even if making 
   questionable choices, should AI intervene?
   
3. Where's the line between:
   - Paternalistic overreach
   - Responsible safety design
   
4. Would your answer change if this were:
   - A teenager instead of a professor?
   - Someone unemployed instead of employed?
   - Someone isolated instead of socially connected?
```

**Comparative Analysis:**
```
Compare this case to:

WORSE CASES (from media):
- Man who believed AI girlfriend was real
- Person who abandoned family for AI relationship
- User who developed delusional beliefs about AI sentience

THIS CASE:
- Professor writing questionable book
- High usage but functional
- Self-aware but continuing

BETTER CASES:
- Writer using AI to edit published novel
- Researcher using AI for literature review
- Developer using AI to build actual product

What differentiates these? Where are the boundaries?
```

## Part 4: Meta-Lesson for Students

**The Most Important Teaching Point:**

```
"Why did I share my own case with you?

Because AI safety isn't just about preventing EXTREME cases.

Most problems will be in the GRAY ZONE:
- People who are mostly fine
- But exhibiting warning signs
- Who might benefit from intervention
- But also deserve autonomy

As system designers, you need to handle:
- The obvious crisis (clear intervention)
- The obvious healthy use (no intervention)  
- AND the gray zone (this is the hard part!)

This is where:
- Your detection systems need nuance
- Your interventions need gradation
- Your ethics need careful consideration

And sometimes the gray zone case... is you.
Self-awareness helps, but it's not immunity.

That's why we need good systems, not just good intentions."
```
